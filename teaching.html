TEACHING INTERESTS

During my doctoral studies at University of Cincinnati, I had the opportunity to teach four different courses as an independent instructor and I was also a teaching assistant (TA) for eight courses led by eight different professors. As a TA, my primary responsibilities involved grading of homework assignments, scheduling office hours and helping students with questions, occasionally delivering lectures, and participating in flipped classroom activities. In the four courses that I taught independently, three were undergraduate level, one course (CS 6051 - Database Theory) had both graduate and undergraduate students.

Guest Speaker

More recently, I was invited (twice) as a guest speaker at Portland State University. My talks were focused on database benchmarking and the idea was to offer students a glimpse into some of the active work in databases from industry perspective. Shared few real-world examples around query optimization, how databases and underlying hardware are connected.

This collaboration with the faculty at PSU (Charles Winstead and Kristin Tufte) further led to a presenatation at a leading CS education conference, ACM SIGCSE 2020.

Preparing Future Faculty

I have also been a part of the Preparing Future Faculty Program and a university-wide teaching and mentoring initiative - Teach Me To Teach at the University of Cincinnati (UC). These programs involved a series of seminars (attended as well as delivered), reading assignments on pedagogical practices as well as teaching observations followed by feedback sessions which were valuable for honing my teaching skills. Professor Karen Davis observed my teaching for a database design course I had taught (IS 330). Additionally, I have been on the executive board of a student-led organization - Graduate Association of Teaching Enhancement (GATE). GATE focuses on providing information, training and instruction on teaching concepts and methods with a focus on innovative pedagogies. During my term in 2013-14, I facilitated and delivered multiple presentations at different workshops at UC [1, 2, 3]. My presentations were focused on the theme of how to become an effective teaching assistant and received an attendance of over 120 graduate students according to the assessment reports generated by CET&L (Centre for Enhancement of Teaching and Learning). CET&L at UC is a comprehensive center with the mission of improving teaching, mentoring and learning for UC faculty and students alike. It spans two courses. ENGR-952:Modern Teaching Techniques and ENGR-953: Mentored Teaching Techniques. The course on Modern Teaching techniques involved creating syllabus and related materials for an undergraduate course and we had in-class discussions on effective teaching techniques, talking about Bloom's Taxonomy and ABET Outcomes. My course was titled "Object-Oriented Programming using Java". This course was being developed as a 400 level course. The course "Mentored Teaching Techniques" calls for a minimum of 10 hours of classroom-teaching. I was able to leverage my teaching experience for the course IS330: Database Design, for business majors,to fulfill the requirement.

Courses taught(as an independent instructor)

  • EECE 493:Software Engineering (Summer 2012)

    Course topics include software lifecycle models, requirements analysis, specification, design, testing, and maintenance. The course introduces students to formal methods and to software engineering management, including global software engineering and ethics.
    The goals of this course are for each student to comprehend the basic principles and practices of contemporary software engineering and to apply them to the process of efficiently developing correct and maintainable large software systems.

  • EECE 495:Software Engineering Lab

    Lab to accompany ECES 493, Software Engineering. Students learn the basics of object-oriented analysis and design, apply concepts taught in Software Engineering lecture, and develop a large group project.

  • CS 6051: Database Theory (Fall 2012)

    The key objectives of this course is to learn principles behind the relational model and further going on to the concepts of normalization. Students will have the opportunity to learn the theoretical principles leveraging relational algebra and relational calculus, which are considered as formal algebraic query languages . The course also involves a presentation with two students per group.

  • IS 4010: Application Development with Java (Fall 2015)

    The key objectives of this course are to introduce students to the principles of object oriented programming paradigm and fundamentals of Java programming. Students will have opportunities to develop and/or improve their software development skills, with strong emphasis on Java language and students will use the integrated development environment - Eclipse. Students will also have the opportunity to learn and build event driven applications in Java using Swing framework. The course will also introduce students to interacting with database (MySQL) programmatically from within Java GUI (Graphical User Interface) applications developed in Swing framework. If time permits and depending upon the interest of the class, there may be a few sessions dedicated to learning web application using Java EE framework (JSP, Servlets).
